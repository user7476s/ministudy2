Chapter 7 — Electric Potential

§7.1Electric Potential Energy

The electrostatic force is conservative, so it has a potential energy. The work done by the field moving a charge from A to B equals the drop in potential energy:

$$ W_{A\to B} = -\Delta U = U_A - U_B $$

For a pair of point charges separated by distance $r$:

$$ U_{12} = \dfrac{k\,q_1 q_2}{r}, \qquad k = 8.99\times10^{9}\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2 $$
  • Sign matters. Like charges ($q_1q_2>0$) → $U>0$ (positive, repulsive). Opposite charges → $U<0$ (bound). This is the whole point of iClicker "same-sign → PE positive."
  • The reference is $U=0$ at $r\to\infty$. Moving a charge from $r_1$ to $r_2$: $\Delta U = kq_1q_2\!\left(\tfrac{1}{r_2}-\tfrac{1}{r_1}\right)$.
  • If $r_2>r_1$, the term $\left(\tfrac{1}{r_2}-\tfrac{1}{r_1}\right)$ is negative — a lecture iClicker favorite.
Electron-volt

1 eV = the energy an electron (or any charge of magnitude $e$) gains accelerating through a potential difference of 1 volt: $1\ \text{eV} = e(1\,\text{V}) = 1.602\times10^{-19}\ \text{J}$. It is a unit of energy, not force or mass.

§7.2Electric Potential & Potential Difference

Potential is potential energy per unit charge — a property of the field, independent of the test charge:

$$ V = \dfrac{U}{q}, \qquad \Delta V = \dfrac{\Delta U}{q} = \dfrac{W_{\text{by field}}}{q}\ \text{(sign care)}, \qquad [V] = \dfrac{\text{J}}{\text{C}} = \text{volt} $$
  • A positive charge speeds up moving from high→low potential (loses PE, gains KE). A negative charge (electron, proton is positive) does the opposite: a proton's KE decreases going low→high voltage.
  • $\Delta(\text{KE}) = q\,\Delta V$. Across 25 V, an electron and proton each change energy by $\pm 25$ eV — but "electron gains 25 J" is false (it's 25 eV, and the sign depends on direction), which is why the iClicker answer was "neither."

§7.3Calculating Potential from Charge

$$ V(r) = \dfrac{kq}{r}\quad\text{(point charge)}, \qquad V = k\sum_i \dfrac{q_i}{r_i}\quad\text{(superposition)}, \qquad V = k\!\int \dfrac{dq}{r}\quad\text{(continuous)} $$
  • $V$ is a scalar — add potentials directly, no vector components. Much easier than adding fields.
  • For a single point charge, $V\to 0$ as $r\to\infty$. So yes, there is a location where the point-charge potential is (approaches) zero: infinitely far away.
  • Dipole: $V$ is positive near $+q$, negative near $-q$, and zero on the perpendicular bisector.

§7.4Determining Field from Potential

The field is the negative gradient of the potential — this is the inverse of integrating $\vec E$ to get $V$:

$$ \vec E = -\nabla V = -\left(\dfrac{\partial V}{\partial x}\hat i + \dfrac{\partial V}{\partial y}\hat j + \dfrac{\partial V}{\partial z}\hat k\right) $$
  • Uniform field / parallel plates: $E = \dfrac{\Delta V}{d}$ (magnitude), field points from high to low potential.
  • iClicker: "potential decreases uniformly east→west" ⇒ $\partial V/\partial x$ constant ⇒ $\vec E$ uniform, pointing west (toward decreasing $V$... field points toward lower potential, i.e. from east high to west low → points west). Uniform because the gradient is constant.
  • The gradient operator's $x$-component is $\hat i\,\partial/\partial x$.

§7.5Equipotential Surfaces & Conductors

  • Equipotentials are surfaces of constant $V$; $\vec E$ is always perpendicular to them.
  • No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential → two points at the same potential have no net field line connecting them along that surface.
  • Point charge: equipotentials are concentric spheres. Infinite line charge: coaxial cylinders. Uniform field: parallel planes.
  • Closer equipotential spacing ⇒ steeper gradient ⇒ stronger field. Equal spacing would mean uniform field — a point charge's equipotentials are not equally spaced, so the "perfect cone" topographic map fails.
  • Conductors (electrostatic): $\vec E = 0$ inside; the entire conductor (surface + interior) is one equipotential; excess charge sits on the surface; $\vec E$ just outside is perpendicular to the surface. "Charge on the surface" does not mean all conduction electrons live on the surface — only the tiny net excess does.
Chapter 8 — Capacitance

§8.1Capacitors & Capacitance

$$ C = \dfrac{Q}{V}, \qquad [C] = \dfrac{\text{C}}{\text{V}} = \text{farad (F)}, \qquad C_{\parallel\text{-plate}} = \dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} $$
  • A capacitor "with charge $Q$" carries $+Q$ on one plate and $-Q$ on the other — net charge zero. "$Q$" refers to the magnitude on each plate.
  • Capacitance depends only on geometry and the dielectric: plate area $A$, separation $d$, and the filling material. It does not depend on the metal type, the applied voltage, or gravitational PE.
  • $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854\times10^{-12}\ \text{F/m}$. Field lines inside a parallel-plate capacitor are perpendicular to the plates, running from the $+$ plate to the $-$ plate.
  • Same $Q$ and same $V$ but smaller $d$ ⇒ larger $E=V/d$ ⇒ stronger field in the narrower gap.

§8.3Energy Stored in a Capacitor

$$ U = \tfrac12 QV = \tfrac12 CV^2 = \dfrac{Q^2}{2C}, \qquad u = \tfrac12 \varepsilon_0 E^2\ \text{(energy density, J/m}^3) $$

The factor of $\tfrac12$ comes from integrating as charge accumulates — you don't move all the charge across the full voltage at once. Energy is stored in the electric field itself.

§8.4Capacitor with a Dielectric

$$ C = \kappa\,C_0, \qquad \kappa \ge 1\ \text{(dielectric constant)} $$

A dielectric raises capacitance by a factor $\kappa$. What happens to $Q$ and $V$ depends on the circuit:

  • Battery disconnected ($Q$ fixed): inserting the dielectric raises $C$, so $V=Q/C$ drops and stored energy decreases (the slab is pulled in).
  • Battery connected ($V$ fixed): $C$ rises, so $Q=CV$ increases and stored energy increases.
  • Dielectrics also raise the breakdown voltage a capacitor can tolerate.

§8.5Molecular Model of a Dielectric

  • An external field polarizes the dielectric — molecular dipoles align (or are induced).
  • The aligned dipoles create bound surface charge whose field opposes the applied field, reducing the net field inside to $E = E_0/\kappa$.
  • Smaller net field ⇒ smaller voltage for the same charge ⇒ higher capacitance. That's the microscopic reason $C=\kappa C_0$.
disconnect-the-battery scenario

A charged parallel-plate capacitor after the battery is removed: the stored charges stay put (nowhere to go until a circuit reconnects), and the field between the plates is static and unchanging. Reconnect it to a load (a tiny resistive heater, say) and current flows — the stored energy discharges and the load does something (warms up). This is the bridge from Ch 8 into Ch 9.

Chapter 9 — Current & Resistance

§9.1Electrical Current

$$ I = \dfrac{dQ}{dt}, \qquad [I] = \dfrac{\text{C}}{\text{s}} = \text{ampere (A)} $$
  • In a metal wire, the actual moving charges are electrons only (the positive ions are locked in the lattice).
  • Conventional current is defined as the direction positive charge would flow — opposite to the electrons' actual drift. It's a sign convention, not literal proton motion.
  • Total charge from steady current: $Q = I\,t$ ⇒ time to move a charge is $t = Q/I$.

§9.2Model of Conduction in Metals

$$ I = n\,q\,A\,v_d, \qquad J = \dfrac{I}{A} = n q v_d\ \text{(current density)} $$
  • $n$ = charge-carrier density, $A$ = cross-section, $v_d$ = drift velocity.
  • Drift speed is tiny — on the order of mm/s (e.g. ~0.77 mm/s), even though the signal and the random thermal speeds are enormous. Electrons don't zip down the wire; the field pushes the whole sea slowly.
  • Number of carriers per second past a point $= I/q$ (e.g. electrons/s $=I/e$).

§9.3Resistivity & Resistance

$$ R = \rho\dfrac{L}{A}, \qquad \sigma = \dfrac{1}{\rho}, \qquad \rho(T) = \rho_0\big[1+\alpha(T-T_0)\big] $$
  • Resistivity $\rho$ is a material property (Ω·m); resistance $R$ depends on the material and geometry (longer → more $R$, thicker → less $R$).
  • For most metals $\rho$ rises with temperature ($\alpha>0$).

§9.4Ohm's Law

$$ V = I\,R \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad R = \dfrac{V}{I} $$
  • A device is ohmic if $R$ is constant — its $I$–$V$ graph is a straight line through the origin. The slope is $1/R$.
  • Non-ohmic devices (diodes, filaments) have curved $I$–$V$ graphs. On an $I$–$V$ plot, pick the linear one as ohmic and read $R$ from its slope.

Worked Examples
§7.1 · potential energy change

Q. $q_1=-0.90\times10^{-5}$ C at the origin; $q_2=+0.70\times10^{-5}$ C moves from $B(23,0)$ cm to $P(0,-29)$ cm. Find $\Delta U$.

Solve. Only the distance from $q_1$ matters. $r_B=0.23$ m, $r_P=0.29$ m. $\Delta U = kq_1q_2\!\left(\tfrac1{r_P}-\tfrac1{r_B}\right)$. Both charges plug in with sign: $q_1q_2=-6.3\times10^{-11}$, and $(1/0.29-1/0.23)=-0.900$. $\Delta U = (8.99\times10^9)(-6.3\times10^{-11})(-0.900) \approx \boxed{+0.509\ \text{J}}$.

§7.1 · work to assemble

Q. Work to bring an electron from infinity to $r=0.529\times10^{-10}$ m from a proton (hydrogen ground state).

Solve. $W_{\text{ext}}=\Delta U = U_f-0 = \dfrac{k(e)(-e)}{r} = \dfrac{(8.99\times10^9)(1.602\times10^{-19})^2}{-\,0.529\times10^{-10}}$ (with opposite signs) $= -4.36\times10^{-18}\ \text{J} = \boxed{-27.2\ \text{eV}}$. Negative → the system releases energy as the electron falls in.

§8.2 · mixed network

Q. $C_{10}=3.1$, $C_{11}=9.9$ (in parallel), then in series with $C_{12}=9.5$ and $C_{13}=7.2\ \mu$F. Find $C_{eq}$.

Solve. Parallel pair: $C_{10}+C_{11}=13.0\ \mu$F. Then all three in series: $\dfrac1{C_{eq}}=\dfrac1{13.0}+\dfrac1{9.5}+\dfrac1{7.2}=0.32108$ ⇒ $C_{eq}=\boxed{3.1145\ \mu\text{F}}$.

§8.1 · Q = CV

Q. A $C=2.2$ nF capacitor at 630 V. Charge stored?

Solve. $Q=CV=(2.2\times10^{-9})(630)=\boxed{1.386\times10^{-6}\ \text{C}}$.

§9.1 · charge and time

Q. A battery moves 6878 C at 0.612 mA. How long does it run?

Solve. $t=Q/I = 6878/(0.612\times10^{-3}) = 1.124\times10^{7}\ \text{s}$ (about 130 days).

§9.3 · resistance of a wire

Q. 10 m of copper wire, 1.0 mm diameter ($\rho=1.68\times10^{-8}$ Ω·m). Resistance?

Solve. $A=\pi(0.5\times10^{-3})^2=7.85\times10^{-7}\,\text{m}^2$. $R=\rho L/A = (1.68\times10^{-8})(10)/(7.85\times10^{-7})\approx\boxed{0.214\ \Omega}$.

Common Traps
  • eV is energy, not force or mass. $E=mc^2$ makes MeV/$c^2$ a mass unit, but MeV alone is energy.
  • $V=0$ does not mean $E=0$ (midpoint of a dipole) and $E=0$ does not mean $V=0$ (inside a charged conductor). Potential and field are related by a derivative/integral, not equality.
  • Capacitance is fixed by geometry. Changing the applied voltage does not change $C$ — it changes $Q$.
  • Series capacitors add reciprocally (opposite of resistors in series). Parallel capacitors add directly.
  • Drift velocity is millimeters per second, not the speed of light — that's the field/signal propagation, a different thing.
  • Conventional current ≠ electron motion. Electrons carry the current in metals but flow opposite to the conventional-current arrow.
  • "Charge on a conductor's surface" refers only to the net excess charge, not every conduction electron.
